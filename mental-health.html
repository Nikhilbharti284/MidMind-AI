Mental Health & Wellness

Understanding, supporting, and nurturing your mental wellbeing

Understanding Mental Health

Mental health includes our emotional, psychological, and social well-being. It affects how we think, feel, and act. It also helps determine how we handle stress, relate to others, and make choices. Mental health is important at every stage of life, from childhood and adolescence through adulthood.

"Mental health is not a destination, but a process. It's about how you drive, not where you're going." — Noam Shpancer, PhD

Many factors contribute to mental health problems, including:

  • Biological factors, such as genes or brain chemistry
  • Life experiences, such as trauma or abuse
  • Family history of mental health problems
  • Lifestyle factors including diet, physical activity, and substance use
Important: Mental health problems are common but help is available. People with mental health problems can get better and many recover completely.

Common Mental Health Conditions

Anxiety Disorders

Anxiety disorders involve persistent, excessive fear or worry in situations that are not threatening. People experiencing anxiety disorders may avoid situations out of worry, experience physical symptoms like sweating and rapid heartbeat.

Types include: Generalized Anxiety Disorder, Panic Disorder, Social Anxiety Disorder, and specific phobias.

Depression

Depression is more than just feeling sad or going through a rough patch. It's a serious mental health condition that requires understanding and medical care. Depression can cause severe symptoms that affect how you feel, think, and handle daily activities.

Types include: Major Depressive Disorder, Persistent Depressive Disorder, Seasonal Affective Disorder, and Postpartum Depression.

PTSD

Post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) can develop after experiencing or witnessing a traumatic event. People with PTSD may have disturbing thoughts and feelings related to their experience that last long after the traumatic event has ended.

Symptoms include: Flashbacks, nightmares, severe anxiety, and uncontrollable thoughts about the event.

Bipolar Disorder

Bipolar disorder causes dramatic shifts in a person's mood, energy, and ability to think clearly. People with this disorder experience emotional highs (mania or hypomania) and lows (depression).

Types include: Bipolar I Disorder, Bipolar II Disorder, and Cyclothymic Disorder.

Mental Health Self-Care Practices

Taking care of your mental health is just as important as caring for your physical health. Here are some evidence-based strategies to nurture your mental wellbeing:

Physical Wellbeing

  • Get regular exercise (aim for 30 minutes daily)
  • Maintain a nutritious diet rich in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, and lean proteins
  • Prioritize sleep quality and aim for 7-9 hours nightly
  • Limit alcohol and avoid recreational drugs
  • Stay hydrated throughout the day

Emotional Wellbeing

  • Practice mindfulness meditation daily
  • Keep a gratitude journal
  • Express emotions in healthy ways (talking, creating art, journaling)
  • Set and maintain healthy boundaries
  • Take breaks when feeling overwhelmed

Social Wellbeing

  • Nurture meaningful relationships
  • Reach out to trusted friends or family when struggling
  • Join community groups or clubs aligned with your interests
  • Volunteer for causes you care about
  • Practice empathy and active listening

Cognitive Wellbeing

  • Challenge negative thought patterns
  • Practice positive self-talk
  • Set realistic goals and expectations
  • Engage in activities that challenge your mind
  • Take breaks from news and social media

When to Seek Professional Help

While self-care strategies are important, sometimes professional help is necessary. Consider seeking help if you experience:

  • Persistent feelings of sadness or hopelessness lasting more than two weeks
  • Overwhelming worry or fear that interferes with daily activities
  • Extreme mood changes
  • Significant changes in eating or sleeping patterns
  • Difficulties performing usual daily functions or responsibilities
  • Withdrawal from friends, family, and usual activities
  • Unusual thoughts, behaviors, or feelings that concern you or others
  • Thoughts of harming yourself or others
Emergency Situations: If you or someone you know is in immediate danger of self-harm or suicide, call your local emergency number (like 911 in the US) or go to the nearest emergency room. For non-emergency situations, contact a mental health professional or call a crisis helpline.

Types of Mental Health Professionals

Different mental health professionals offer different types of services:

Psychiatrists

Medical doctors who can diagnose mental health conditions, prescribe and monitor medications, and provide therapy. They specialize in mental health and can help determine if symptoms have a physical cause.

Psychologists

Professionals with doctoral degrees (PhD, PsyD) who can diagnose conditions and provide therapy. They specialize in psychological testing and evidence-based therapy approaches but typically cannot prescribe medication.

Therapists & Counselors

Mental health professionals (including licensed clinical social workers, licensed professional counselors, and marriage and family therapists) who provide therapy and counseling to help people address emotional, behavioral, or mental health concerns.

Primary Care Providers

Physicians, nurse practitioners, and physician assistants often serve as the first point of contact. They can help with initial assessment, prescribe some medications, and refer to specialized mental health providers when needed.

Common Treatment Approaches

Mental health treatment is often personalized to the individual, and may include:

Psychotherapy (Talk Therapy)

Talking with a mental health professional to develop coping strategies, process feelings, and change behavior patterns.

Types include: Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT), Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT), Psychodynamic Therapy, and Interpersonal Therapy.

Medication

Psychiatric medications work by regulating brain chemicals that affect mood and thoughts. They are often used alongside therapy.

Types include: Antidepressants, anti-anxiety medications, mood stabilizers, and antipsychotics.

Lifestyle Changes

Modifications to daily habits that support mental wellness, often recommended alongside other treatments.

Examples include: Regular exercise, improved sleep hygiene, nutritional changes, stress reduction techniques, and social connection.

Support Groups

Groups where people with similar experiences meet to share challenges and successes, provide emotional support, and exchange practical information.

Can be in-person or online, peer-led or professionally facilitated.

Supporting Someone with Mental Health Challenges

If someone you care about is struggling with their mental health, here are ways to help:

  • Learn about their specific mental health condition
  • Express your concern and willingness to help
  • Listen without judgment or trying to "fix" the problem
  • Encourage them to seek professional help and offer to help them find resources
  • Continue including them in activities and social events, even if they often decline
  • Be patient and understand that recovery takes time
  • Take care of your own mental health while supporting others
"The most basic and powerful way to connect to another person is to listen. Just listen. Perhaps the most important thing we ever give each other is our attention." — Rachel Naomi Remen
